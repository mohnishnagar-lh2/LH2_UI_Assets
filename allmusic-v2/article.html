FOO FIGHTERSYour Favorite Toy
Album Review

Your Favorite Toy

Foo Fighters

★★★★★
RCA Pop/Rock 2026 Editors' Choice

Review by Neil Z. Yeung · April 24, 2026

Share

Brisk and invigorated, the Foo Fighters' twelfth studio album finds the veteran band in full rock mode, offering a breathless half-hour of energetic fun that ranks among their tightest work in years.

After the introspective sprawl of 2023's But Here We Are, the Foo Fighters return with a record that feels almost like a corrective: ten songs in thirty-two minutes, no filler, no detours into ambient interludes. The riffs come fast, the hooks come faster, and Dave Grohl sings like a man rediscovering exactly why he started a band in the first place.

Opener "Static City" sets the tone — a propulsive, three-chord thrash that wouldn't have sounded out of place on The Colour and the Shape, but with the production heft only thirty years of stadium experience can deliver. From there, the album barely lets up. "Headlamps" rides a Pat Smear riff that earns every second of its 2:48 runtime. The title track turns a deceptively simple verse-chorus structure into something genuinely thrilling by the time the final chorus arrives.

What's most surprising about Your Favorite Toy isn't its quality — at this point, a tight Foo Fighters record is what we've come to expect — but its sense of joy. This is a band having fun, leaning into their identity rather than questioning it, and the listener gets to ride along.

"Ten songs in thirty-two minutes, no filler, no detours. The riffs come fast, the hooks come faster."

Drummer Josh Freese, in his first full studio outing with the band, plays with a brawler's instinct that suits these songs perfectly — never showy, always serving the groove. Producer Greg Kurstin keeps the mix wide and bright, giving every guitar layer room to breathe without sacrificing the punch.

The mid-album ballad "Slow Burn" is the only real pause for breath, and even it earns its place by the time the final guitar solo lands. By the closer, "Last One Out," you're ready to flip the record and start again. That's the highest praise you can give a rock album in 2026 — that it leaves you wanting more.

Your Favorite Toy isn't reinventing anything. It doesn't have to. It's the sound of a band that knows exactly what it does well, and decided to do exactly that, with maximum conviction. That, somehow, has become the rarest thing in rock right now.

About the Band

Foo Fighters formed in 1994 as a Dave Grohl solo project after the dissolution of Nirvana. Over twelve studio albums, they have become one of the defining American rock bands of the 21st century.
